01   Research & Discovery

Understanding the real problem before designing anything

Before opening Figma, I spent time understanding how students actually behave when they get stuck on a problem. I conducted interviews and observation sessions with students, teachers, and academic coordinators across middle and high school grades. The goal wasn't to validate a solution — it was to understand the friction points in the current learning experience.

"I just Google the answer. By the time I find a hint that actually makes sense, I've already lost interest in the problem."

This pattern kept surfacing. Students weren't lazy — they were hitting dead ends with no support structure, and the path of least resistance was to abandon the problem entirely. I interviewed 12 students, 4 teachers, and 2 academic coordinators over two weeks, and shadowed two classroom sessions to observe how students interacted with the existing LMS during assignments.

Key research findings

Finding 01

8 out of 12 students abandoned a problem within 2 minutes if they had no idea where to start. There was no in-context scaffolding in the LMS to help them re-engage.

Finding 02

Students found generic hints ("remember to use the formula") useless. What worked in classrooms were personalised analogies from teachers who knew what the student cared about.

Finding 03

Teachers weren't worried about students getting hints — they were worried about students getting answers. The distinction between guidance and solution was the core tension.

Finding 04

Students responded significantly better when they felt in control of the pacing. Being pushed through steps too quickly felt like being lectured. They wanted to set the tempo.

02   Pain Points & How Might We

Turning what I heard into design questions

After synthesising research notes, I mapped recurring frustrations into clear pain points, then reframed each one as a "How Might We" question to open up the solution space before converging on a direction.

Pain points

P1

No in-context help

Students had nowhere to turn inside the LMS when stuck. External resources broke focus entirely.

P2

Hints feel irrelevant

Generic examples didn't connect with students' personal frame of reference, making them harder to absorb.

P3

Loss of agency

Being walked through steps without control made students feel talked at, not supported.

P4

Anxiety when stuck

Students associated asking for help with failure. Fear of judgement made them avoid help-seeking entirely.

How might we

From P1How might we bring guidance directly into the problem-solving context without breaking the student's flow?
From P2How might we make hints feel like they were written specifically for this student, not for a textbook?
From P3How might we give students control over when and how fast they receive help?
From P4How might we make asking for help feel like a smart, normal part of the learning process?

The three student choices — Help me, Next step, More clarity — were the result of several IA iterations. Early versions had two options, but testing revealed students needed a middle state that wasn't "I'm completely lost" but also wasn't "I'm ready to move on."

04   Wireframes & Prototypes

The product in context

I moved from IA to low-fidelity wireframes, focusing on the core interaction loop: how students access Newton, how the conversation panel sits within the LMS, and how guidance is delivered without obscuring the problem. After three rounds of internal review, I moved to high-fidelity screens across web and mobile.

Embedded chat panel

Newton sits alongside the assessment — not over it. The split layout was a deliberate IA decision: students always keep the problem in view while reading hints.

Subject selection

Designed for low friction. Upload detection handles subject categorisation automatically — students confirm in one tap rather than navigating a settings flow.

Mobile companion

Mobile required a different layout strategy — the chat takes full focus while the problem is accessible via a persistent bar. Same information, different hierarchy.

The hardest wireframing challenge wasn't the chat UI — it was deciding what Newton should never show. Every design review came back to the same question: "are we too close to giving the answer here?"

05   Key Design Decisions

The choices that shaped the experience

These were the decisions that had the most impact on the final product — and where I had to make a clear case internally for one direction over another.

Panel, not modal

Interface architecture

An early concept placed Newton in a modal overlay. I pushed for a side panel instead — students need the problem in view while reading hints. Hiding it forced unnecessary context switching between problem and guidance.

Impact

Students in testing could reference the problem and hint simultaneously — a behaviour that didn't happen at all with the modal prototype.

Three choices, not two

Interaction design

The initial flow had two options: "Help me" and "Next step." Testing showed students often wanted clarification without advancing. Adding "I need more clarity" gave them a middle path and reduced drop-offs significantly.

Impact

Drop-off rate within the first two interactions decreased noticeably after introducing the third option in UAT sessions.

30-second nudge, not instant

Timing & pacing

I tested nudge timings at 10, 20, and 30 seconds. 10 seconds felt intrusive — students reported feeling rushed. 30 seconds gave enough space to think. The nudge was written to feel like a friendly check-in, not a system alert.

Impact

Students who received nudges at 30 seconds re-engaged at a significantly higher rate than those in the 10-second test group.

Auto subject detection

Onboarding flow

Early designs required manual subject selection before Newton could help. I redesigned this to auto-detect from uploaded files, then ask for a single confirmation tap — removing a 3-step flow and replacing it with one.

Impact

Time to first Newton interaction dropped significantly when auto-detection replaced the manual selection flow in the revised prototype.

Tone as a design tool

Content & voice

I wrote and tested multiple tones — neutral, encouraging, and witty. Neutral felt cold. Witty tested best with middle school students. I worked with the content team to establish a voice guide that used students' personal interests as a hook for every analogy.

Impact

Students in testing described the personalised tone as "actually helpful" and "not like a robot" — two of the most common qualitative responses.

06   Design System & Components

Building consistency across the experience

Newton sits inside an existing LMS, so the design system had to extend Argus's visual language without competing with it. I focused on defining the components specific to Newton's conversational UI — the parts that didn't exist in the base system yet.

07   Usability Testing & Iterations

What broke, what changed, what I learned from users

I ran two rounds of usability testing — formative on wireframes with 6 students, and summative on the high-fidelity prototype with 8 students and 3 teachers. Sessions were moderated, task-based, and recorded. I gave participants a real assessment task and observed how they interacted with Newton without prompting.

Issue found

Students didn't notice the Newton icon in the header. 4 out of 6 participants in round one never initiated a session unprompted.

Design change

I moved the Newton entry point adjacent to each question — a contextual trigger right where students are already looking when they get stuck.

Issue found

Two students thought "Show me the next step" meant they'd be shown the answer. The label caused anxiety — they didn't want to cheat.

Design change

Relabelled to "Guide me to the next step" in round two. The misunderstanding disappeared entirely in subsequent testing.

Issue found

On mobile, the chat panel covered the problem completely. Students had to scroll repeatedly to reference the question.

Design change

Redesigned mobile layout to use a persistent collapsible problem bar at the top — always visible, expandable with one tap.

Key insight

Teachers were more concerned about Newton's tone than its accuracy. "If it sounds harsh, students will close it immediately."

Key insight

Students who used "I need more clarity" twice in a row were consistently the most engaged by session end — not the least.

What I'd do differently

  • I'd involve teachers much earlier. Their mental model of "helpful" was fundamentally different from students' — and it shaped tone decisions more than any student interview did.
  • The personalisation system was designed with depth but launched with limited interest data. A lightweight 3-question preference screen at sign-up would have improved the first session significantly.
  • I underestimated how much the mobile layout differed in cognitive load. I'd treat mobile as a separate design track from day one — not an adaptation of desktop.
  • The "tone as a design tool" insight came late. I'd establish voice and content principles before designing any screens — they constrain interface decisions in ways I didn't appreciate until mid-project.
  • I'd design an educator-facing view as part of the initial scope. Teachers needed visibility into student progress, and its absence in v1 meant they couldn't use Newton as a teaching tool.
